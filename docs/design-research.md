# Design notes

Pattern of One was shaped by interactive art projects where the visitor can understand the cause and effect quickly, but the result still feels personal to that moment.

## References

- [Universal Everything — Future You](https://www.universaleverything.com/art/future-you): a visitor’s movement can make a form feel present without turning it into a portrait of their body.
- [Google Creative Lab — Move Mirror](https://experiments.withgoogle.com/move-mirror): explain the input simply and reward the first movement quickly.
- [Memo Akten — Learning to See](https://www.memo.tv/works/learning-to-see-interactive/): computational interpretation should stay visibly conditional, not pretend to be objective truth.
- [Patatap](https://patatap.com/): immediate links between action, motion, and sound can make a small interaction feel alive.
- [Species in Pieces](https://species-in-pieces.com/): one consistent visual language can carry a whole project.
- [Bruno Simon](https://bruno-simon.com/): practical controls such as sound and quality settings should be easy to find when an experience asks for attention.

## What carried into this project

- Start with a simple form and let it change through one short encounter.
- Keep the canvas central and the interface quiet.
- Make camera and microphone use optional, explained, and easy to leave.
- Use one restrained mark-making system instead of a collection of unrelated effects.
- Treat the result as an artistic response to the session, not a claim about the participant.

## What I avoided

The project deliberately avoids literal bodies or faces, pose-matching, dashboard-like signal displays, heavy 3D scenes, and language that makes the system sound more certain than it is.
